Exploring Different Yoga Styles

By Charlotte posted March 27, 2018

In this article I explain why I value both consistency and variety in my yoga practice.

Recently, I've been thinking a lot about which yoga style is my favourite or which would describe best my way of practising yoga. I've mainly trained in Hatha and Ashtanga Vinyasa; however, over the years I've practised with various teachers and looked into quite different styles of yoga. I feel that it's great to have a strong foundation in the traditional schools to be able to understand the origins of yoga. In my teacher training, for example, we put great emphasis on yoga philosophy and anatomy which provides a solid base for my practice. 

For my own approach, I've realised that it can be helpful to have a fixed routine, a set of asanas and breathing exercises which I include in every session. Repeating this set constantly enables me to grow further and to explore deeper. It gives me a better understanding of how my body changes during the different poses. This is also one reason why I understand the beauty of Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga: The fixed sequence can help me switch off the mind and master the asanas well over time.

As much as I value consistency and repetition, I also believe that variety is important when practising yoga. I love experimenting with different styles and with a more 'flowing' approach to the poses. I feel that it's beneficial to explore how the body can move more freely in any pose. It's also fun to try out new elements, to challenge body and mind. Many 'Vinyasa Flow' or 'Hatha-inspired' yoga classes offer this more fluent approach to yoga.

Finding a good balance between variety and consistency is a good step towards including yoga naturally in our own lives. After all we're never the same persons from one day to the next. We might need more restorative elements at one moment while seeking a challenge when we're bursting with energy. Playfully exploring new approaches can also help overcome a fear of failure and approach life with more ease. Yoga should support us during these endeavours by being both an anchor and a tool to experiment with our body movements.
